My Process

Discover

I believe great products solve real problems. And real problems are discovered through intimate knowledge of the people experiencing them. This knowledge can be gained in many ways depending on the timeline and demands of the project.

Ideally, I love to interview people. Want to create an app to help pool owners manage their pool chemistry? Talk to pool owners. Find out about the learning curve, the pain points, any hacks they've created, and what they enjoy.

Other methods of research include contextual inquiry (great for observing real behavior), qualitative and quantitative surveys, competitive analysis, analytics, focus groups, and subject-matter experts. The tools I use depend on time, budget, and resources available.

Design & Test

Once a problem has been identified, I hold ideation sessions with the team to foster divergent thinking. These ideation sessions include people from different backgrounds (developers, project managers, content writers, etc.). I use creative exercises (such as Crazy Eights) to get as many ideas as possible.

Once the ideas have been accumulated, I wireframe different solutions and create prototypes for usability testing. Getting feedback from real users helps me narrow down the range of possible ideas and focus on the best ones.

I follow the Nielson Norman mantra of four to six users per round of usability tests. This allows us to discover most of the issues. After the first round I make updates to the prototypes to fix obvious issues. One to two more rounds of tests with prototype updates in between results in a solution that is solid.

Evaluate & Iterate

Once the MVP is released, I look at analytics to see where improvements can be made. I advocate for UX improvements to be prioritized to enhance the experience. I also look for customer feedback for ideas and features.

A temptation of clients and product owners is to pack in as many cool features as possible that didn't make it into the MVP. But the ideal method is to prioritize fixes, enhancements and features based on the user's needs and goals.

Fixes are solutions to the usability problems that slipped through testing undiscovered. To understand what fixes need to be made, there are a variety of methods. Contextual inquiry, interviews, surveys, and analytics. Usability tests can also be conducted at this stage.

Enhancements are the V2 version of your MVP features. An example would be a cool transition or animation that helps the user make a connection between two objects (a basic example is an add to cart animation to bring the user's attention to the cart icon). Enhancements can be disovered through any of the above methods.

And finally features that didn't make the MVP cut need to be prioritized. The key to a great V2 is to balance fixes, enhancements, and new features. In my experience, it is tempting for clients and product owners to fixate on new features, often times neglecting fixes and enhancements. But a good UX process can help create balance.
